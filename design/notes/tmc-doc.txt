Project Abyssal Trust
Cisco Consulting Proposal for The Metals Company (TMC)
Bat Bridge Partners — Cisco HS Capstone 2026
PART 1: THE PROBLEMS
Before any solution, here is an honest inventory of what is actually wrong at TMC. This ordering
matters: judges reward teams that diagnose before they prescribe.
Problem 1 — The Trust Deficit (the root problem)
TMC's single greatest business risk is not technical. It is that nobody believes their
environmental claims. Their pre-feasibility study was written largely by their own employees
rather than independent experts. Their "zero waste" claim excludes the sediment-laden water
they discharge back into the ocean. Greenpeace, the Environmental Justice Foundation,
MiningWatch, and the International Seabed Authority itself have all publicly attacked their
credibility. When your regulator, your critics, and independent analysts all distrust your data,
every permit, every investor dollar, and every insurance policy gets harder and more expensive.
Key insight our solution is built on: TMC does not have a mining problem. It has a proof
problem.
Problem 2 —1960s Collection Technology
TMC's collector vehicles are conventional: tracked machines and suction systems whose basic
architecture has barely changed in sixty years. They cannot distinguish a bare nodule from a
nodule hosting living organisms, and they generate large sediment plumes — the two specific
harms critics cite most. This is simultaneously their biggest ethical liability and our biggest
opportunity, because it means the AI maturity of their core operation is near zero. Everything we
propose is additive. Nothing is ripped out.
Problem 3 — An Unsecured, Geopolitically Exposed Operation
TMC operates in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone under a U.S. permit that the ISA calls a violation
of international law — meaning multiple nation-states and activist groups have motive to disrupt,
discredit, or surveil the operation. Their attack surface is enormous and almost entirely

operational technology (OT): a production vessel functioning as a floating industrial plant,
remotely operated and autonomous underwater vehicles, a satellite link to shore, and an
environmental sensor network whose data feeds directly into regulatory decisions.
The nightmare scenarios are specific: a hostile actor spoofs or corrupts environmental
monitoring data (either to trigger a false violation that kills the license, or to hide a real one and
destroy TMC's credibility when discovered); ransomware locks the vessel's industrial controls
mid-operation, hundreds of miles from help; or vessel-tracking (AIS) manipulation near the
operating area creates collision risk or false narratives. The Baltic Sea taught the world in
2024–25 that maritime infrastructure incidents are cheap to cause and nearly impossible to
attribute. TMC is a higher-profile, more hated target than a telecom cable.
Problem 4 — Financial Fragility Constrains Everything
TMC posted a net loss of roughly $320M in 2025 and independent analysts question whether
the project can ever be profitable. Any solution costing "whatever it takes" is dead on arrival.
The proposal must be phased, must lead with the components that unlock revenue and
licensing (not vanity tech), and must show a defensible return story.
Problem 5 — Regulatory Dependency on Data Integrity
NOAA's license comes with environmental compliance conditions. In this industry, the
environmental data pipeline IS the license. If that pipeline is not provably trustworthy —
tamper-evident, auditable, chain-of-custody intact — then a single disputed dataset can
suspend operations. Today, no deep-sea miner treats environmental telemetry as
security-critical infrastructure. That is the gap.
PART 2: THE SOLUTION — "THE GLASS OCEAN"
The Big Idea (our differentiator)
Every other team will propose AI + firewalls to make their company more efficient. We propose
something different: turn TMC's environmental monitoring into a radically transparent,
cryptographically verifiable public utility — the Glass Ocean — secured end-to-end by
Cisco infrastructure.
Instead of TMC saying "trust us," the seafloor broadcasts its own evidence. Environmental
sensor data is signed at the source, transported over a secured Cisco OT network, logged
immutably, and exposed on a public dashboard that regulators, scientists, and yes, even

Greenpeace can watch in near-real-time. TMC's biggest liability (distrust) becomes its moat: the
first extraction company whose environmental claims are independently verifiable by design.
This reframes the entire project: security isn't a cost center protecting mining. Security is the
product that makes the mining licensable.
AI Use Case 1 — Selective Harvesting Retrofit ("Eyes on the
Collector")
Computer-vision perception retrofitted onto TMC's existing collector vehicles. Cameras and
lights feed an onboard AI model that classifies nodules in the vehicle's path in real time: bare
nodule (harvest) versus nodule with visible epifauna — sponges, corals, xenophyophores (skip).
The collector head adjusts pickup on the fly. A second model monitors the sediment plume live
via turbidity sensors and optical backscatter, automatically throttling vehicle speed when plume
density exceeds a threshold.
Why this is additive, not replacement: the vehicles, the ship, the riser system all stay. We are
adding a perception layer to machinery that currently operates blind — the industrial equivalent
of adding driver-assist to an existing truck fleet. (We deliberately cite Impossible Metals'
selective-harvesting robots as proof the concept works, then differentiate: they built a new robot;
we retrofit intelligence onto proven heavy equipment a real company already owns.)
Where the AI runs: on the vessel, at the edge. A shipboard Cisco UCS / AI POD-class compute
node does inference locally because (a) there is nowhere near enough satellite bandwidth to
stream 4K seafloor video to a cloud, and (b) a 700ms+ satellite round-trip is useless for a
vehicle decision that must happen in tens of milliseconds. Sustainability pillar bonus: edge
inference slashes satellite bandwidth (energy + cost) and lets us use pre-trained vision
backbones fine-tuned on nodule imagery rather than training from scratch.
AI Use Case 2 — The Environmental Sentinel Network ("The
Ocean's Black Box")
A distributed AI monitoring system that is deliberately independent from the mining control
system: moored sensor strings and monitoring AUVs measuring turbidity, sediment plume drift,
bioacoustics (marine mammal calls, ambient soundscape), water chemistry, and periodic
photographic biodiversity transects. Onboard AI does three jobs: plume-drift prediction (a
physics-informed model forecasting where sediment travels over 6–48 hours so operations can
be adjusted before a violation, not after), bioacoustic event detection (whale presence
auto-triggers operational quieting protocols), and anomaly detection on the sensors themselves
(an AI watching for sensor drift, gaps, or statistically implausible readings — i.e., tampering or
failure).

This second use case is what feeds the Glass Ocean. And critically for the ethics rubric: the
Sentinel Network's data is treated as evidence, engineered to a chain-of-custody standard, not
as internal telemetry.
The Cisco Stack (front to back)
On the seafloor and in the water column: sensor pods and AUVs are, in network terms,
untrusted industrial endpoints. Their data lands at the vessel.
Honest engineering note we volunteer before judges catch it: radio waves do not propagate
underwater. Subsea links use acoustic modems, tethers, or optical links — none of which are
Cisco products. Cisco's role begins the moment data reaches a surface asset. Claiming
otherwise would be the "space-hardened router" mistake, and we refuse to make it.
On the surface: Cisco Ultra-Reliable Wireless Backhaul (CURWB) connects the production
vessel to its support craft, surface buoys (the tops of moored sensor strings), and USVs — this
is CURWB's actual designed use case (ports, mining, moving industrial assets). Onboard the
vessel, Cisco Catalyst Industrial Ethernet switches form the ship's OT backbone.
Segmentation — the architectural heart: Cisco Identity Services Engine (ISE) plus industrial
firewalling divides the vessel into strict zones: (1) vessel IT / crew network, (2) mining OT
(collector controls, riser, dynamic positioning), (3) the Environmental Sentinel network, and (4)
the satellite uplink DMZ. The Sentinel zone is one-way-out by design (data diode pattern):
environmental data can leave, but nothing on the ship — including TMC's own operators — can
write into it. That single design choice is what makes "the miner can't fake its own environmental
data" a provable architectural claim instead of a policy promise. Cisco Cyber Vision passively
inventories and monitors every OT device in zones 2 and 3, flagging anomalous industrial
commands (the Ascension lesson: 7 compromised servers took down a 25,000-server network
because nothing was segmented; here, a breached crew laptop can never reach a collector
PLC).
Identity and access: Cisco Duo enforces phishing-resistant MFA for every remote operations
session and every shore-side engineer — directly closing the MGM lesson (one
social-engineered helpdesk call) and the Colonial lesson (one password, no MFA). Shore-based
remote access flows through Cisco Secure Access (ZTNA), not a flat VPN.
Ship-to-shore and visibility: Cisco ThousandEyes monitors the satellite path and shore
connectivity end-to-end — when the link degrades mid-Pacific, operators know in seconds
whether the fault is shipboard, satellite, or shore. Cisco Umbrella provides DNS-layer protection
for all shipboard internet egress (the Ascension entry vector: one malicious download). Cisco
XDR correlates detections across endpoint, network, and identity; Splunk (a Cisco company
— a point most teams won't know) serves as the immutable, timestamped audit log for
both security events and the environmental data ledger.

The Glass Ocean layer: every Sentinel reading is hash-chained and signed as it enters Splunk,
producing a tamper-evident ledger. A public dashboard publishes near-real-time plume,
acoustic, and biodiversity indicators, with a cryptographic proof page that lets any third party
verify the data hasn't been altered since capture. A standing Webex-based "regulator
observation room" gives NOAA and invited independent scientists live windows into operations
— turning oversight from an adversarial audit into a continuous feed. (Webex is a deliberately
unglamorous inclusion that scores "use of Cisco technologies" breadth honestly.)
Value & Success Metrics (the pillar TMC's finances make hardest
— so we quantify around trust, not tonnage)
The honest framing: our solution does not make a marginal mine profitable. It makes an
unlicensable operation licensable and an uninsurable operation insurable. Concrete metrics:
percentage of fauna-hosting nodules avoided (target from pilot data, e.g., >90% detection
recall); plume-threshold exceedance events per 1,000 operating hours (target: trending to zero
with predictive throttling); mean time to detect an OT anomaly (from "never/unknown" to minutes
via Cyber Vision + XDR); environmental data disputes resolvable by ledger proof (target: 100%);
avoided downtime cost (using CommonSpirit's ~$160M and Ascension's ~$1.1B as sector
analogies for what one extended OT outage costs a mid-cap operator — an offshore vessel
idled by ransomware burns charter, crew, and schedule costs daily); and unlocked capital —
ESG-screened investors and insurers currently priced out by unverifiable claims. One honest
caveat we state on the slide: several of these are risk-avoidance and optionality values, not
guaranteed revenue, and we defend that openly rather than inventing fake ROI percentages.
Sustainability & Deployment (rubric pillar 4)
Edge-first inference on shipboard UCS (bandwidth and energy efficiency; the satellite link is the
scarcest resource in the architecture). Pre-trained vision models fine-tuned on nodule imagery,
not trained from scratch. Hybrid hosting: heavy model retraining happens onshore between
voyages; only lightweight model updates ship over the link. Hardware is containerized and
modular so the same stack redeploys across future vessels — and, in a licensing twist that
addresses TMC's cash problem, the Glass Ocean architecture itself is packageable as a
compliance product TMC could license to other seabed operators, converting a cost center into
a potential revenue line.
Implementation Roadmap (phased for a cash-poor client)
Phase 0 (months 0–3): security fundamentals only — Duo, Umbrella, segmentation design,
Cyber Vision passive discovery. Lowest cost, closes the MGM/Colonial/Ascension class of
failure immediately. Phase 1 (months 3–9): vessel OT segmentation implemented; Sentinel
network pilot with hash-chained logging; ThousandEyes on the uplink. Phase 2 (months 9–18):

PART 3: FRONT-TO-BACK AUDIT (attacking our
own solution before the judges do)
"Why would a company losing $320M/year buy any of this?" Strongest objection, so it's
first. Answer: Phase 0 costs a rounding error against one incident, and Phases 2–3 are gated
on financing milestones. Deeper answer: TMC's constraint on raising money IS the trust deficit;
the Glass Ocean is pitched as the thing that unlocks capital, not a luxury purchased after capital
arrives. If judges still push: we note the architecture is client-portable to any seabed operator
(AOMC/Odyssey, national programs), so the design's value doesn't die with TMC's balance
sheet.
"Isn't consulting for the ocean's most-protested company unethical?" We answer
head-on: the capstone brief says the client wants to modernize "safely & ethically" — our
proposal is the mechanism that forces that, since a one-way data architecture removes TMC's
ability to lie. If TMC operates under Glass Ocean, the world gets the first verifiable evidence
base about deep-sea mining impacts; if the data shows unacceptable harm, that evidence
empowers regulators to stop it. We are not defending seabed mining; we are making it
impossible to do in the dark. (We should also acknowledge the counterargument —
transparency can legitimize an activity that shouldn't happen at all — and say the team debated
it. Judges reward visible ethical reasoning over tidy answers.)
"Underwater comms aren't Cisco — doesn't that hole the story?" No, because we scoped it
honestly: Cisco owns surface-and-up plus everything shipboard and shoreside, which is where
every one of the named failure modes (ransomware, spoofed data, stolen credentials, flat
networks) actually lives. Volunteering the limitation is itself a Technical Feasibility point.
"Can computer vision really run on a collector at 4,000m depth?" Cameras, LED lighting,
and edge inference on subsea vehicles are established (Impossible Metals does
classification-grade vision on nodules today; offshore inspection ROVs run CV routinely). Our
retrofit claim is deliberately conservative: advisory mode first, human-in-the-loop, automatic
control only in Phase 3. We do not promise a percentage improvement we can't source.

"The Glass Ocean could backfire — real-time data might hand critics ammunition."
Correct, and that's the point; we say so. A company only adopts this if it intends to operate
within limits. The predictive-throttling design exists precisely so TMC sees a violation coming
before the public feed shows one. Risk remains that leadership rejects transparency — which is
a client-adoption risk, not a design flaw, and we flag it as the project's #1 non-technical risk.
"AIS spoofing and hostile vessels — you mentioned the threat, where's the counter?"
Scoped honestly: shipboard radar/AIS cross-checking with anomaly detection is feasible and
included in the Sentinel anomaly layer; actual interdiction of hostile vessels is a state function,
not a Cisco function. We recommend TMC formalize information-sharing with NOAA and naval
authorities — the Baltic Sentry lesson.
Remaining weaknesses we accept: no historical breach at TMC means our impact numbers
borrow from healthcare/energy analogies (we label them as analogies); public sentiment risk is
real and some judges may simply dislike the client; and the Glass Ocean's originality cuts both
ways — it invites more scrutiny than a generic SOC-and-firewalls proposal would. We take that
trade. The rubric pays 30 points for originality and this is the most original defensible position on
the board.
One-slide summary (for the team)
Client: The Metals Company. Diagnosis: a proof problem wearing a mining costume.
Prescription: retrofit intelligence onto blind machines (AI #1), build an incorruptible
environmental witness (AI #2), and secure it all with a segmented, zero-trust Cisco architecture
whose signature feature — the one-way Glass Ocean — turns the company's worst liability into
its license to operate.

This is a genuinely good instinct — you're describing a platform play: the Glass Ocean stops
being TMC's compliance tool and becomes industry infrastructure. That's exactly what
"scalability" means to a Cisco judge. But I need to push back hard on one sentence before it
infects your deck, then I'll show you how to structure both ideas so they strengthen rather than
dilute.
The pushback first: kill the phrase "take the data and do almost anything with it." Never
say anything like that in the room. Your entire core solution is built on the claim that this data is a
trust instrument — tamper-proof, governed, evidence-grade. The moment you say "we can use
the data any way that benefits us," you've contradicted your own thesis in front of judges, and
the ethics pillar collapses. Worse, "monetize environmental data however we want" is precisely
the behavior (Kaiser's tracking-pixel breach) that we cited as a cautionary tale earlier. The
correct framing — and it's more impressive, not less — is governed data products: specific,
named uses under a published data-governance policy. Same ambition, defensible packaging.
Now the two satellite solutions, structured properly:
Ring 1 — "SafeDepth": Deep-Sea Health & Safety Platform. Extend the same shipboard
architecture to human safety: connected crew wearables and vessel sensors (man-overboard
detection, gas/atmosphere monitoring in enclosed spaces, fatigue-shift analytics for 24/7 remote
operations, emergency comms redundancy over the CURWB mesh), all riding the network
zones you already built — no new infrastructure, just a new tenant on it. The scalability
sentence writes itself: every offshore operator (mining, wind, oil & gas, cable repair, research
vessels) has identical crew-safety obligations, so SafeDepth is sellable to the whole maritime
industry regardless of whether deep-sea mining itself survives. That's a subtle but powerful
hedge: your solution outlives your controversial client.
Ring 2 — "Abyssal Weather": Ocean Data as Public Infrastructure. Your Sentinel Network is
already measuring currents, temperature, turbidity, and acoustics at 4,000m in one of the
least-instrumented regions on Earth. The deep Pacific is a data desert — oceanographers and
climate modelers would genuinely value continuous abyssal time-series. So: feed governed,
anonymized-of-operations oceanographic data to NOAA, universities, and climate models; use
current-prediction internally to optimize vessel positioning, plume forecasting (this directly
boosts your core AI Use Case 2 — better current data = better plume prediction = fewer
violations), and weather-window planning that cuts fuel burn (sustainability pillar). Commercially,
ocean-current and weather data has real buyers — shipping route optimization, offshore energy
— as a data-as-a-service line under the governance policy. Frame it as "the first commercial
abyssal observation network — mining pays for the sensors, science gets the data."
How to present this without diluting the core: use a concentric-rings slide. Center: Glass
Ocean + two AI use cases (TMC-specific). Ring 1: SafeDepth (same architecture, new safety
tenant, industry-wide market). Ring 2: Abyssal Weather (same sensors, new data products,
societal benefit + revenue hedge). One slide, 60 seconds of talk time, and it hits Scalability,

Sustainability, and Long-term Benefits simultaneously. Do not give the rings more than that —
your 100 points live in the core solution, and teams that sprawl across three half-built ideas
score worse than teams with one deep idea and two crisp extensions. The rings exist to answer
